Home is far more than a physical house. It is the papaya tree planted by a grandparent, the cemetery where generations rest, the shoreline where children learn to fish, and the neighbors who know the histories behind every doorway.

Today, climate change dictates where people can live. Pacific Island communities flee inland to escape storm surges, wrestle from the wreckage of repeated extreme weather events, and confront the agonizing choice to stay or abandon ancestral soil.

Some move once. Others move again and again.

This story traces that journey — from climate disasters to displacement, through the cascading health and economic crises that follow, and finally to the Pacific innovators leading locally-led climate adaptation solutions.

When Home Moves

Across the Pacific, climate disasters force people to flee their homes. Some communities rebuild. Others relocate. All redefine what it means to adapt

By Elham Ali

Chapter 1

A crisis measured in millions

The Pacific contributes almost nothing to the climate crisis, yet it experiences some of its most severe consequences.

Pacific Island Countries and Territories contribute a minuscule 0.02% of global greenhouse gas emissions. Yet they endure the world’s most severe climate penalties: rising sea levels, warming oceans, extreme flooding, and rapid coastal erosion. The inequity is starkest for low-lying atoll nations like Kiribati and Tuvalu, which account for less than 0.003% and 0.0002% of global emissions, respectively.

By comparison, high-income nations like Aotearoa New Zealand emit 43 times Tuvalu’s total output, and a staggering 650 times the emissions of Kiribati.

Between 2008 and 2025, climate-driven and geophysical disasters directly affected more than 2.5 million people across 21 Pacific nations, based on data compiled from the Pacific Data Hub.

Fiji, for example, has suffered the largest cumulative affected population in the region over this period, totaling 1,240,734 people.

The single largest reported country-year disaster impact in regional history occurred in Fiji in 2016, when a catastrophic cyclone season directly affected 633,584 people — striking roughly 60% to 62% of the nation's entire population.

Cyclone Winston, a historic Category 5 storm, drove that destruction and permanently scarred the landscape.

Between 2008 and 2025, the Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre (IDMC) recorded 247 distinct disaster events in the Pacific, triggering nearly one million internal displacement movements.

Visualization forthcomingNearly one million displacement movements

These figures count displacement movements rather than unique individuals. A single person can be forced to flee their home multiple times. The vast majority of these individuals remain within national borders as internally displaced persons (IDPs). However, the threat is escalating, with extreme weather putting at least 50,000 Pacific Islanders at risk of losing their homes every single year, as tracked by the IDMC Pacific Response to Disaster Displacement Project.

Chapter 2

What Forces People to Leave?

The overwhelming driver of Pacific displacement is weather-related disasters rather than geophysical ones.

In February 2016, Tropical Cyclone Winston was the most powerful cyclone ever recorded in the Southern Hemisphere, the first-ever Category 5 storm to strike Fiji, and the second strongest storm to make landfall anywhere on Earth, with sustained wind speeds exceeding 280 km/h.

In a matter of days, it affected approximately 62% of Fiji's total population, according to a study published by the UCL Faculty of Mathematical & Physical Sciences. It also triggered 62,000 internal displacement movements.

The physical damage to infrastructure was severe because traditional homes in rural and maritime areas are typically constructed from wood and corrugated iron. These materials proved incapable of withstanding Category 5 forces. Compounding this vulnerability, the vast majority of rural residents lacked any form of property or crop insurance, leaving them entirely dependent on external aid.

Just four years later, Tropical Cyclone Harold (2020) struck Fiji, Vanuatu, and Tonga, displacing over 10,000 people and complicating recovery efforts as communities struggled under the restrictions and economic strains of the COVID-19 pandemic.

Gradual climate degradation can merge with historical geostrategic exploitation to create existential threats just as terrifying as a Category 5 cyclone. In the Marshall Islands, slow-onset sea-level rise is structurally fused with the toxic legacy of U.S. nuclear weapons testing.

Between 1946 and 1958, the United States detonated 67 nuclear weapons across Bikini and Enewetak Atolls. This included the infamous 1954 “Castle Bravo” thermonuclear blast—a 15-megaton explosion 1,000 times more powerful than the bomb dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki combined. Bravo blanketed 11,000 square kilometers in lethal radioactive fallout, rendering entire islands uninhabitable and forcing the displacement of indigenous populations.

To contain the resulting radioactive debris, the U.S. military bulldozed 90,000 cubic meters of contaminated soil into an atomic blast crater on Runit Island, capping it with an 18-inch-thick concrete dome. To save time and expense, the bottom of the crater was never lined with concrete.

Today, as sea levels rise, high tides wash directly through the unlined bottom of the crater, flushing radioactive Plutonium-239 and uranium straight into Enewetak's lagoon and the North Equatorial Current, with Marshall Islands plutonium detected over 2,800 miles away. With severe typhoons threatening to breach the cracked concrete cap entirely, the Runit Dome stands as a ticking nuclear-climate time bomb—and an environmental injustice threatening to trigger a second wave of displacement.

Chapter 3

When Disaster Doesn't End

Displacement from climate disasters in the Pacific is not a single, isolated event; it is a chronic, recurring cycle that continually resets national development.

Papua New Guinea leads the region with 81 distinct displacement events spanned across 15 separate years. The nation ranks 16th out of 191 countries globally for overall disaster risk on the INFORM Risk Index, prone to multiple natural hazards, such as floods, droughts, and cyclones.

Visualization forthcoming Papua New Guinea’s recurring displacement events

Papua New Guinea lacks the systemic cushion to withstand these blows, ranking 10th globally for its severe lack of coping capacity. When disasters hit, both individual citizens and the national government lack the fiscal reserves required to build back resiliently. Between 2008 and 2022, the country was battered by 54 reported major disaster events, generating over 282,000 internal displacements.

In the short period between 2016 and 2021, Pacific nations endured 13 extreme weather events, three of which were the worst on record in regional history. Globally, the average gap between billion-dollar disasters has shrunk from 82 days in the 1980s to just 19 days in the 2020s, as reported by Council Fire and the World Resources Institute (2025). This leaves communities with virtually no time to rebuild before the next strike.

Chapter 4

What Happens After People Leave?

Displacement and forced migration from climate disasters compound a cascade of socio-economic and health vulnerabilities.

Economic toll

Climate disasters inflict severe financial damage that cripples regional economies. Fiji reported around $374M in direct disaster economic loss for 2016 — the year Cyclones Winston and Xena and a December tropical depression were the dominant disasters — in the UNISDR/Sendai Framework country-year data analyzed. The Fijian government estimated that the impact of Cyclone Winston amounted to $900 million USD, more than 20% of the country's GDP.

Visualization forthcoming Direct disaster economic losses across Pacific nations

During Tropical Cyclone Harold (2020), border closures had already decimated the tourism industry—the lifeblood of the Fijian economy—causing a 20% contraction in Fiji's GDP. Climate scientists call these “cascading vulnerabilities” — where a second disaster strikes a population whose defenses are already depleted.

This precarity is mirrored across the region: Vanuatu's 2018 dual volcanic and earthquake crisis caused a sudden direct economic loss of $64,500,000, while Tonga's 2014 Cyclone Ian displaced 5,300 people and generated an instant loss of $18,560,063.

Visualization forthcoming The Pacific climate adaptation debt loop

Pacific Island nations are currently forced to expend up to 30% of their entire national budgets solely on climate adaptation, such as constructing seawalls, building embankments, and desalinating water. Low-lying island nations must borrow massive sums relative to their small economies to build vital roads, bridges, schools, and hospitals. When a disaster strikes, it dismantles that investment, trapping the nation in an inescapable debt loop.

A global retreat in aid worsens this financial precarity. Findings from the Lowy Institute Pacific Aid Map indicate that total official development finance to the Pacific has contracted by 16%, driven by deep funding cuts from traditional Western donors. The United Kingdom slashed global aid by 40%, while the United States implemented an estimated $60 billion cut to international development programs.

TB incidence

In the Pacific, 90% of the population lives within five kilometers of a coastline and more than a quarter resides within a single kilometer.

Forced to abandon sinking outer islands, communities are pushed into centralized urban refuges. In South Tarawa (Kiribati), over 60,000 people are crammed into a tiny 15-square-kilometer strip of land. Households average more than 7 people, and it is common for 15 to 40 people to live squashed together in tiny, two-room traditional dwellings. This extreme density facilitates the rapid spread of airborne infectious diseases, like Tuberculosis (TB).

Pacific Data Hub findings reveal that the Marshall Islands has the highest TB rate in the region at 692 per 100,000 people in 2023—representing an alarming 723.8% long-run surge from its rate of 84 in 2000. Kiribati follows at 533 per 100,000 (+50.1% since 2000), and Tuvalu stands at 296 per 100,000, both all massively exceeding the regional median of 48.0 per 100,000.

Unsafe drinking water

Low-lying coral atolls have no rivers; they rely on thin underground freshwater lenses and rainwater harvesting. Rising sea levels and King tides cause severe coastal erosion and saltwater intrusion, poisoning fragile underground freshwater resources.

Analysis from Pacific Data Hub on safe drinking water reveals that Papua New Guinea has the lowest safely managed water access in the region, with nearly half the population relying on unsafe water as of 2022.

The Solomon Islands shows a devastating 11.23 percentage point long-run decline in safe water access (falling from 78.68% in 2000 to 67.45% in 2021). In Kiribati, the 24.31% unsafe-water gap drives high waterborne disease outbreaks, directly causing Kiribati to suffer from the highest infant mortality rates in the Pacific.

Undernourishment

Saltwater intrusion and E. coli saturation poison agricultural soil, making it impossible to cultivate traditional crops like taro, banana, and breadfruit. Stripped of subsistence agriculture, displaced communities rely on expensive, imported, low-nutrient, and highly processed canned foods like canned spam. This transition has triggered an undernourishment epidemic.

FAO estimates that approximately 28.7% of Papua New Guinea’s population had habitual dietary energy consumption below minimum requirements, as of 2023.

The nation exhibits severe exposure to intense heatwaves, exemplified by the massive 2015–2016 El Niño event. The resulting severe drought triggered a catastrophic famine impacting 1.3 million people, leaving 480,000 citizens in a state of critical food shortage. The Solomon Islands witnessed its undernourishment rates nearly double, spiking from 11.2% to 20.0%, as of 2023.

Visualization forthcoming Undernourishment trends across the Pacific

This dietary shift has triggered a metabolic crisis: 82% of Pacific adults are overweight or obese, 33% suffer from hypertension, and up to 40% live with high cholesterol. Crucially, resulting metabolic diseases like diabetes compromise immune systems, completing the tragic biological loop by drastically increasing susceptibility to active Tuberculosis.

Chapter 5

Fighting Back

Amidst relentless environmental shocks, Pacific Island communities are fighting for their land.

They do so despite an international legal vacuum: the 1951 Refugee Convention does not recognize environmental or climate-induced displacement as valid grounds for refugee status, and strict, unceasing immigration frameworks, notably New Zealand's Acceptable Standard of Health (ASH) rules offer no sanctuary. Under ASH, resident visa applicants are rejected if they are deemed to place a high demand on health services, and immigration instructions dictate that medical waivers will not be granted to anyone who has or has had different forms of tuberculosis under certain conditions.

The Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre (IDMC) tracks these local strategies. Across the region, communities are deploying localized, nature-based, and structural frameworks to secure their territory on their own terms.

Source: IDMC Good Practices.

Fiji is a pioneer, establishing a comprehensive 130-page blueprint, the Standard Operating Procedures (SOP) for Planned Relocations, to govern how to move endangered populations. Relocation is treated as a strict measure of last resort; the government must exhaust all engineering adaptation options (dredging rivers, raising homes on stilts, building seawalls) before approving a move.

Mixed results of early relocations informed the SOP's foundational rules. In 2014, Vunidogoloa became the first climate-relocated village in Fiji. About 140 residents moved two kilometers inland. While safe, they faced severe “loss of adjacency” to their ancestral burial grounds, and had to travel much further to access their traditional fishing reefs. Because only male leaders were consulted, the newly built homes completely lacked kitchens.

Tukuraki was devastated by a 2012 landslide that buried 50% of the village and killed a family of four. While survivors lived in makeshift tents, their temporary infrastructure was flattened again by Cyclone Evan (2013) and Cyclone Winston (2016), forcing them to seek shelter in caves before finally relocating to a resilient site in 2017. However, the absence of formal financial contracts sparked social friction, as the modern amenities provided to Tukuraki exceeded the living standards of the host community.

To rectify these blunders, Fiji's updated SOP mandates an unprecedented 90% consent threshold across every single demographic group — including men, women, the elderly, youth, LGBTQ+ residents, and disabled residents. At present, close to 50 Fijian villages are earmarked for potential relocation, with six already moved.

Moving entire communities is an immense financial challenge. In the pending relocation of Nabavatu village, 85 families (over 460 people) have been forced to live in temporary disaster tents for over five years. The cost of physically moving Nabavatu alone exceeds $2.5 million USD.

To address this, Fiji launched the world's first legislated national trust fund dedicated to planned relocation: the Climate Relocation of Communities (CROC) Trust Fund Act 2019, funded locally through a 3% contribution from Fiji's Environmental and Climate Adaptation Levy (ECAL) on the tourism sector and luxury goods, and supplemented by bilateral partners like New Zealand (which provided an initial NZ$2 million).

At a regional scale, this ingenuity has scaled into The Pacific Resilience Facility (PRF)—a Pacific-owned, grant-based regional fund designed to bypass the rigid, slow, and bureaucratic administrative hurdles of massive global financial institutions.

Research from the World Resources Institute (WRI) and Council Fire proves that funding these Pacific-led frameworks is a remarkably high-yield investment. WRI's research highlights that every $1 invested in climate adaptation yields $10.50 in net economic benefits over a 10-year horizon.

Proactive disaster risk management projects also yield an average 36% return on investment. This triple-dividend model works because the commercial, social, and environmental gains unlocked on a daily basis — such as local job creation, agricultural productivity, and ecosystem health—far exceed the value of avoided losses alone. The investment pays off continuously, even if a disaster never strikes.
